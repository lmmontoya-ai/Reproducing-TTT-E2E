# Draft v1: Warm-Starting TTT-E2E for Long-Context Language Modeling

This draft is written as a paper-style narrative for the completed `125M` study. All empirical claims in this version are therefore limited to `125M`.

## Abstract

Long-context language modeling requires a model to use substantially more context without losing the strong next-token prediction behavior that makes short-context pretraining effective. Test-Time Training End-to-End (TTT-E2E) is attractive in this setting because it does not rely solely on larger attention caches; instead, it compresses context into temporary weight updates while retaining the standard language-model objective. In this paper, we study whether TTT-E2E should be trained from scratch for long-context use, or whether it can be warm-started effectively from a shorter-context full-attention seed. We reproduce the `125M` regime of the TTT-E2E program in a local JAX implementation aligned to the original architectural and training recipe, and organize the study as a controlled ladder with four roles: full-attention continuation, naive SWA conversion, warm-started TTT-E2E, and from-scratch TTT-E2E. To make the comparison scientifically interpretable, we enforce durable stage completion, rerun evaluation from saved checkpoints with corrected float32 aggregation, and add two continuation ablations: one that asks whether plain continuation can drive the warm-start branch up to the scratch branch’s quality, and one that equalizes total branch tokens in favor of scratch TTT-E2E. The results are consistent across all completed comparisons. Warm-started TTT-E2E substantially improves over both full-attention continuation and naive SWA conversion, making it a strong practicality result. However, from-scratch TTT-E2E is the best final-quality model at `125M`: the final `32K` Books validation loss is `3.2729` for scratch TTT-E2E versus `3.9173` for the warm-start branch. Continuing the warm-start branch for `+1440` extra steps still fails to match the scratch baseline, while equalizing total branch tokens improves scratch TTT-E2E further to `3.2623`. At `125M`, warm-start is therefore best understood as a practical route to a strong long-context model, but not as the final-quality winner.

## I. Introduction

The practical challenge in long-context language modeling is not simply to extend sequence length, but to do so without breaking the optimization behavior that makes modern language models effective in the first place. Standard full attention offers strong short-context modeling, but its latency and memory costs become increasingly unfavorable as context grows. This has motivated a large class of alternatives that attempt to replace or approximate attention. TTT-E2E is interesting because it approaches the problem from a different direction: instead of treating long context only as a sequence-layer design problem, it treats context as self-supervised training data and uses gradient-based online adaptation to store useful information in temporary weight changes.

This framing makes TTT-E2E scientifically compelling, but it also complicates evaluation. A warm-started TTT-E2E model may inherit strong priors from its initialization. A from-scratch TTT-E2E model may benefit from training under its own objective from the beginning. A long-context extension may fail because of systems constraints rather than because of the method itself. As a result, a single favorable endpoint is not enough to answer the core research question. We need a design that makes the baselines explicit, fixes the evaluation surface, and keeps the comparison interpretable when budgets differ across branches.

Our study does this through an experimental ladder at `125M`. The ladder starts from short-context seeds and asks four progressively stronger questions. First, what happens if a full-attention model is simply extended to `32K`? Second, what happens if the same seed is converted naively to a sliding-window regime? Third, what happens if the seed is first adapted into the TTT-E2E regime and only then extended to `32K`? Fourth, what happens if the model is trained as TTT-E2E from scratch? This ladder isolates the scientific role of warm-start rather than collapsing all long-context strategies into one comparison.

The ladder alone, however, does not settle the fairness question. The warm-start branch and the scratch branch are matched on the final `32K` extension budget, but they do not consume the same total branch tokens because the warm-start path includes an extra bridge stage. For this reason, we add two continuation ablations after finishing the main ladder. One asks whether simple continued training can bring the warm-start branch up to scratch quality. The other asks whether scratch TTT-E2E still wins when it is granted the same total token budget used by the warm-start branch. Together, these experiments let us distinguish between a practical advantage and a genuine final-quality advantage.

The main contributions of this paper are:

- A paper-faithful `125M` reproduction of the full-attention, SWA, warm-started TTT-E2E, and from-scratch TTT-E2E comparison under a single controlled experimental ladder.
- A measurement protocol that validates stage completion from durable artifacts and re-evaluates saved checkpoints with corrected aggregation, so the reported numbers are tied to reproducible checkpoints rather than transient runtime output.
- A complete `125M` result showing that warm-started TTT-E2E is a strong improvement over simpler long-context baselines, but that from-scratch TTT-E2E remains the best final-quality model.
- Two continuation ablations that sharpen the interpretation: plain continuation does not allow the warm-start branch to catch scratch TTT-E2E within a substantial additional budget, and equalizing total branch tokens still favors scratch TTT-E2E on final quality.

## III. Problem Statement and Solution Overview

The central problem in this work is not whether TTT-E2E can be trained at all, but how to compare warm-start and scratch TTT-E2E in a way that supports defensible scientific claims. A naive comparison between the final warm-started `32K` model and the final scratch `32K` model is informative, but incomplete. It answers which endpoint is better under the default recipe, yet it leaves open whether the difference is due to better optimization, different total token budgets, or simply insufficient continuation of the warm-start branch.

We address this with a two-part study design. The first part is a controlled ladder that organizes the long-context problem into four experimental roles. The full-attention continuation branch isolates what is obtained from extending the original seed directly. The naive SWA branch tests whether replacing the sequence mechanism alone is sufficient. The warm-start branch tests whether an `8K` full-attention seed can be converted into a strong `32K` TTT-E2E model through an intermediate bridge stage. The scratch branch measures what the same TTT-E2E family can achieve when trained on its own path from the beginning.

The second part of the design is a pair of continuation ablations. The iso-quality ablation continues the finished warm-start branch under the same optimizer state, learning-rate tail, data, batch size, and context length. Its purpose is to ask a narrow but important question: if we simply keep training the warm-start branch, does it converge to the same quality as scratch TTT-E2E? The iso-total-tokens ablation continues the scratch branch by exactly the extra upstream token budget consumed by the warm-start path. Its purpose is to answer the fairness question that the main ladder cannot answer on its own: if both branches are compared at equal total branch tokens, does scratch TTT-E2E still hold the quality advantage?

The result is a study that supports three complementary interpretations. The main ladder measures the practical outcome of the recipes as they would actually be used. The iso-quality ablation measures how expensive it is to repair any warm-start deficit through plain continuation. The iso-total-tokens ablation measures whether the scratch advantage survives a fairer total-budget comparison. This structure allows us to tell a much more precise story than a single endpoint result would permit.

## IV. Proposed Solution

Our proposed solution adopts the TTT-E2E view that long-context language modeling should be handled by combining local sequence modeling with online adaptation, rather than by scaling attention alone. The model keeps a Transformer-style backbone with sliding-window attention for strong local context, while a restricted suffix of the network is updated through gradient steps taken on the ordinary next-token objective as the sequence is processed. In this sense, the model does not merely read context; it also learns from it on the fly. Training is end to end because the outer objective optimizes the model for its post-update behavior, so the model is explicitly trained to be a good initialization for its own inner-loop adaptation.

Within this framework, our warm-start strategy is straightforward. Instead of training the entire long-context TTT-E2E system from scratch, we first obtain a competent short-context full-attention model. We then examine three ways of extending it. The first is the simplest possible control: continue the full-attention model directly to `32K`. The second is a mechanism-only baseline: swap to a sliding-window style extension without a learned TTT-E2E bridge. The third is the warm-start TTT-E2E path: adapt the full-attention seed into the TTT-E2E regime at `8K`, and only then extend it to `32K`. Finally, we compare all of these with a model trained as TTT-E2E on its own path from scratch.

This organization is important because it isolates distinct hypotheses. If naive conversion were already strong, then a warm-start bridge might be unnecessary. If warm-start were enough to match scratch TTT-E2E, then the main contribution of scratch training would be practicality rather than quality. If warm-start improved substantially over simpler baselines but still failed to catch scratch, then warm-start would be best understood as a practical compromise rather than a replacement for scratch training. The continuation ablations are designed precisely to resolve these possibilities without changing the method itself.

## V. Implementation

The experiments were implemented in the local reproduction runtime rather than by executing the read-only reference runtime directly. The intent was not to reproduce the original codebase byte for byte, but to reproduce the architecture, data mapping, and optimization recipe faithfully enough that the resulting scientific comparison would remain aligned with the original TTT-E2E program. All implementation work therefore lives in the reproduction repository, with the reference snapshots used only as read-only guides for alignment and audit.

The `125M` study uses two data regimes. Pretraining and bridge adaptation are performed on `dclm_filter_8k` at `8K` context, while long-context extension is performed on `books3` at `32K`. The four final `32K` comparison stages all use the same direct extension budget: `480` steps, `global_batch_size = 8`, and `seq_length = 32768`, corresponding to `125,829,120` extension tokens per stage. The `8K` pretraining stages use `global_batch_size = 64`, `seq_length = 8192`, and `4800` steps, while the intermediate warm-start bridge uses a shorter `8K` budget. This design ensures that the final `32K` stages are compared on the same downstream task surface even though the full branch histories differ.

To keep the results publication-grade, we treated reproducibility and measurement as part of the experimental method. Each stage was considered complete only when it satisfied a durable artifact contract: successful training output, a valid checkpoint, a successful parity evaluation, and a verified export manifest. This prevented transient runs or partially written artifacts from being mistaken for valid stage results. After the ladder was completed, we also re-evaluated the saved checkpoints using corrected float32 metric aggregation, because earlier BF16-heavy aggregation could quantize final scalar summaries too coarsely. The numbers reported in this draft therefore come from saved checkpoints and corrected eval-only reruns, not from re-training the completed ladder.

The continuation ablations were executed as a separate lineage so that the main `125M` results would remain frozen. The iso-quality continuation resumes from the final warm-start `32K` checkpoint with full optimizer state and continues in `360`-step blocks, evaluating every `120` steps up to a hard cap of `+1440` extra steps. The iso-total-tokens continuation resumes from the final scratch `32K` checkpoint and runs for `+960` extra steps, exactly matching the additional upstream token budget used by the warm-start branch. In both cases, the evaluation surface remains the same corrected `Books32K` validation setup used in the main ladder.

## VI. Results (What We Got So Far)

### Main Ladder at `125M`

The main `32K` comparison is clear. The full-attention continuation branch (`S0_125M`) reaches a final `Books32K loss_ce_mean` of `6.583984375`, and the naive SWA conversion branch (`S1_125M`) improves only marginally to `6.5418243408203125`. By contrast, the warm-started TTT-E2E branch (`S2_125M`) reaches `3.917266845703125`, showing that warm-start is a large qualitative step beyond either simpler extension strategy. The scratch TTT-E2E branch (`S3_125M`) performs best overall at `3.2729225158691406`.

These numbers answer the first practical question directly. Warm-started TTT-E2E is not a cosmetic improvement; it substantially outperforms both full-attention continuation and naive SWA conversion. Relative to `S1_125M`, the warm-start branch improves by `2.6246` absolute loss, or about `40.1%`. However, the second question has a different answer. Warm-start is not the final-quality winner at `125M`. The scratch branch improves over the warm-start branch by `0.6443` loss, or about `16.45%` relative to the warm-start endpoint.

The `8K` upstream stages help interpret this result. The full-attention `8K` pretraining stage (`S0_PRETRAIN_FA_125M`) reaches `3.524169921875` on held-out `DCLM 8K`, while the scratch TTT-E2E `8K` pretraining stage (`S3_PRETRAIN_E2E_125M`) reaches `3.481170654296875`. These short-context results are close. The large difference emerges at the long-context `32K` stage, where the downstream extension path matters much more than the initial `8K` loss alone.

### Cost and Practicality

The practical argument for warm-start is real, but it must be stated carefully. If a strong full-attention seed already exists, then the marginal warm-start path consists of the bridge stage plus the final `32K` TTT-E2E extension. That path costs `3.4656` GPU-hours, whereas the scratch TTT-E2E path costs `25.7073` GPU-hours. On this marginal view, warm-start is about `7.4x` cheaper. This is a genuine practical advantage and one of the strongest positive results of the `125M` study.

The end-to-end comparison is less dramatic. If the cost of training the original full-attention seed is also included, then the full warm-start branch costs `22.6082` GPU-hours, compared with `25.7073` GPU-hours for the full scratch TTT-E2E branch. Warm-start remains somewhat cheaper, but the advantage narrows substantially. At `125M`, warm-start is therefore best interpreted as a route to a strong model at lower marginal cost, not as a route to the absolute best model at the same final quality.

### Why the Canonical Comparison Is Not Fully Apples-to-Apples

The main `S2` versus `S3` comparison is matched on the final `32K` evaluation surface and on the direct extension budget, but it is not matched on total branch tokens. The warm-start branch consists of `S0_PRETRAIN_FA_125M + S2_ADAPT_125M + S2_125M`, for `2,894,069,760` total tokens. The scratch branch consists of `S3_PRETRAIN_E2E_125M + S3_125M`, for `2,642,411,520` total tokens. In other words, the warm-start branch actually consumes `251,658,240` more tokens and still ends worse than scratch. This asymmetry does not invalidate the canonical endpoint comparison, but it does mean that the fairest interpretation requires additional experiments rather than a single endpoint claim.

### Continuation Ablation: Can Warm-Start Catch Scratch by Simply Training Longer?

The iso-quality ablation addresses the most charitable version of the warm-start argument: perhaps the warm-start branch is simply undertrained, and plain continuation would eventually bring it to scratch quality. To test this, we resumed `S2_125M` exactly as it stood, without resetting optimizer state, changing the schedule, altering the data, or modifying the recipe. The target remained the scratch branch’s held-out `Books32K` loss of `3.2729225158691406`.

The answer is negative. Over `+1440` extra continuation steps, corresponding to `377,487,360` additional tokens, the warm-start branch improved monotonically but only slightly. The held-out frontier moved from `3.9171371459960938` at step `480` to `3.8657073974609375` at step `1919`. The total gain relative to canonical `S2_125M` was only `0.0516` loss, or about `1.32%`, while the remaining gap to scratch TTT-E2E was still `0.5928`. This continuation consumed `5.2112` additional GPU-hours, bringing the full warm-start branch to `27.8194` GPU-hours. The implication is strong: plain continuation does not rescue the warm-start branch. At least under the original recipe and optimizer trajectory, the warm-start model does not appear to be on a realistic path to the scratch endpoint.

### Continuation Ablation: What If Scratch Receives the Same Total Token Budget?

The iso-total-tokens ablation asks the complementary fairness question. Since the warm-start branch used an extra bridge stage, what happens if scratch TTT-E2E is given exactly the same total branch token budget? We therefore continued the final scratch `32K` checkpoint for `+960` extra steps, corresponding to the same `251,658,240` extra tokens consumed by the warm-start bridge stage.

Here the answer is also clear. The scratch branch improved steadily from `3.272876739501953` at step `480` to `3.2623252868652344` at the final equal-total-tokens endpoint at step `1439`. This endpoint is better than canonical scratch TTT-E2E by `0.0106` loss and better than canonical warm-start by `0.6549` loss. In other words, once total branch tokens are equalized, scratch TTT-E2E still remains the quality winner, and in fact strengthens its lead slightly. The price is compute: the equal-total-tokens scratch branch reaches `28.4501` GPU-hours in total, which is `5.8419` GPU-hours more than the full warm-start branch.

### Interpretation

The completed `125M` evidence supports a precise narrative. Warm-start is undeniably useful: it produces a much stronger `32K` model than either full-attention continuation or naive SWA conversion, and it does so at very attractive marginal cost when a suitable full-attention seed already exists. However, that practical success should not be conflated with final-quality parity. In every completed comparison, scratch TTT-E2E remains the best final-quality model: at the default endpoint, after substantial continuation of the warm-start branch, and after equalizing total branch tokens in favor of scratch.

This is therefore not a story in which warm-start “almost matches” scratch with less compute. It is a story in which warm-start offers a strong and useful practical compromise, while scratch TTT-E2E remains the highest-quality path at `125M`. That distinction is scientifically important because it separates two claims that are often blurred together in long-context work: the claim that a method is easier or cheaper to deploy, and the claim that it is ultimately the best model. At `125M`, only the first of those claims holds for warm-start. The next step is to determine whether the same pattern persists at the larger `760M` scale.
